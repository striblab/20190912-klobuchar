How Minnesota boosts its homegrown presidential campaign

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune September 12, 2019

Sen. Amy Klobuchar joins a narrowed field of presidential candidates on Friday night's primary debate stage, and has reached this point partially due to strong support from her Minnesota constituents.

Each new debate round triggers tightened DNC donor and polling requirements, which has winnowed the roster by half so far. Candidates with strong bases of small donors – those who give $200 or less to a campaign – tend to move forward, even with flagging poll numbers.

Klobuchar's resilience through 2019's front half and first two debates relied upon a significant pool of Minnesotans donating modest amounts to her campaign, often through ActBlue, a partisan non-profit with a rising profile as a convenient way to give money to Democratic organizations.

Here are some ways Minnesotans have helped keep Klobuchar in the mix:

Minnesota's small donors choose Klobuchar

Klobuchar has run on her ability to connect with midwestern voters, including in places Trump claimed in 2016. With her campaign's hopes pinned on February's Iowa Caucus and March's Super Tuesday states (that include Minnesota), strong fundraising from voters in those states could signal possible future successes.

And while small donor data suggests some indecision among Minnesota Democrats as two dozen presidential candidates jockey for support from a pivotal battleground prize, Klobuchar maintains a healthy statewide lead in donations, cash and unique donors statewide, even over Sen. Bernie Sanders (I-Vt.), who overwhelmingly won the state caucus in 2016.

And while about a fifth of Klobuchar’s roughly 20,000 unique donors also gave to other candidates, the other 80 percent gave exclusively to her campaign, suggesting some home terf loyalty that not all candidates have.

Minnesota is Klobuchar's top donor source

Along those lines, a quarter of Klobuchar's 79,000 small donors come from Minnesota, more than any single state. Minnesota also accounts for nearly a third of her $9 million in small donations.

Though Klobuchar on average polls similarly to some candidates absent from Friday night’s debates, her donor count has helped keep her aloft, even as others have failed to qualify or dropped out entirely – meaning Minnesota has an outsized impact on her campaign's continued survival.

Klobuchar helps drive Minnesota's early interest

With the flurry of debates, advertisements and two dozen candidates running around state fairs, it's easy to forget it's still early in the primary season, yet Democrats appear far more engaged than previous cycles.

And Klobuchar has captured a significant portion of that enthusiasm, as her numbers alone match the entirety of 2015's ActBlue small donations to presidential candidates from Minnesotans.

Increased donor engagement
Comparing ActBlue donations to presidential campaigns from Minnesotans between 2015 and 2019 shows an uptick in small donations to Democrats, with Klobuchar already matching all of 2015's activity.

Minnesotans also responded most to Klobuchar after her snowy February presidential campaign announcement and around her appearance in June's first Democratic primary debate.

Democrats outpacing last cycle's engagement
Minnesota ActBlue donations to all Democratic organizations in the first six months of 2019 outpaced 2015, with donation boosts to Klobuchar appearing at key times.

Klobuchar has also qualified for October's debate with the necessary polls and donors. Future FEC filings will reveal whether she's managed to expand her donor base outside Minnesota.
